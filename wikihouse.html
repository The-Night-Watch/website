Digital and print design guidelines

WikiHouse

WikiHouse is an initiative from London based non-profit Open Systems Lab. They developed an open source, zero-carbon timber building system that allows anyone to build a home using their library of modules. The modules, made from CNC machined plywood sheets, can be made on-site before assembly.

I was tasked to create the design guidelines of the new version of their building system, including the layout and design language for the construction manuals.

My work was focused on creating a coherent design system for their documentation that spanned all supported media: web, mobile, and print. Great care was taken to offer optimal legibility and consistency on each medium, so that the reading experience could be started on one platform and picked up on another with minimal friction.

One interesting aspect of the project was to design instructions for a wide range of users, from beginner customers to construction professionals, through the use of clear and familiar iconography, and a visual-first approach for the assembly manual.

A significant part of my time was spent on research. In my effort to make WikiHouse's design language as clear as possible, I wanted to really grasp what made instructions approachable and universal.

Lego and Ikea instruction manuals were an obvious inspiration, but it wsa only the beginning of my research. I thouroughly analysed the 1965 British Rail Corporate Identity Manual, paying particular attention to the signage guidelines. Rail companies need to convey critical information in a time sensitive context, which usually leads to effective design. I also dived into the history of Prague's tram network signage. The system was put in place in the 1900s, when most people couldn't read, so the entire design system was color and symbol based, which I thought was a great example of universal design.
